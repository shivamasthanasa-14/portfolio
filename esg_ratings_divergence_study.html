ESG Ratings Divergence Study

How MSCI, Sustainalytics, and CDP rate the same European companies differently and why those differences matter for investors.
Companies: Shell, Volkswagen, Nestlé, Danone, Siemens, BASF, Unilever
Focus: Methodology divergence, ranking shifts, investor implications

Introduction

ESG ratings are widely used in sustainable investing, yet the same company can receive sharply different assessments depending on the rating provider. This study compares seven major European companies across MSCI, Sustainalytics, and CDP to understand why these differences arise and what they mean for investors.

The findings show that divergence is not random noise but a result of fundamentally different methodologies, data inputs, and definitions of ESG performance. In practice, that means provider choice can directly shape portfolio construction, risk screening, and the interpretation of sustainability leadership.

Executive takeaway

ESG ratings should not be treated as a single source of truth.

The same company can shift from laggard to leader depending on whether the framework emphasizes relative peer performance, unmanaged absolute risk, or environmental disclosure quality.

Methodology

This study compares publicly available ESG ratings for seven European companies across MSCI, Sustainalytics, and CDP. Because the three providers use different scales, ratings were converted into a common 0–100 format for visual comparison. MSCI letter ratings were mapped to a numeric scale, Sustainalytics risk scores were inverted so that higher values indicate stronger ESG performance, and CDP scores were converted from letter grades into numeric equivalents.

The purpose of normalization is visual comparability, not to imply that the three systems measure the same underlying concept.

ESG rankings change depending on who you ask

Volkswagen is the clearest example of rating divergence in this sample. It ranks last under MSCI, improves under Sustainalytics, and moves into the leadership band under CDP. This shift is large enough to change portfolio inclusion decisions.

ESG ratings rarely agree with each other

MSCI shows weak alignment with both Sustainalytics and CDP, while Sustainalytics and CDP are more closely aligned in this sample. This reflects fundamental methodological differences rather than simple data inconsistencies.

Why the ratings diverge

Different questions

MSCI asks how resilient a company is to ESG risks relative to sector peers. Sustainalytics asks how much unmanaged ESG risk the company carries in absolute terms. CDP focuses on environmental disclosure quality and evidence of climate action.

Different scope

The providers do not cover the same issues with the same depth. CDP is heavily environmental, Sustainalytics captures broader unmanaged ESG risks, and MSCI emphasizes financially material issues by industry.

Different weighting

Governance controversies, pollution exposure, climate strategy, supply-chain management, and sector risk are weighted differently across methodologies. That can materially change the final signal investors receive.

Case studies

Volkswagen

Volkswagen shows the sharpest divergence in the sample. MSCI continues to penalize the company heavily, reflecting the long shadow of Dieselgate and governance concerns. Sustainalytics is more moderate, while CDP places Volkswagen much higher due to stronger environmental disclosure and climate-related progress.

Shell

Shell illustrates the difference between relative and absolute ESG logic. Even when it performs better than sector peers on disclosure and transition planning, it still carries substantial real-world transition risk due to fossil-fuel exposure.

BASF

BASF performs strongly in climate-related disclosure, which supports a high CDP score. At the same time, chemical-sector exposures such as pollution, hazardous materials, and regulatory risk weigh more heavily in broader ESG risk frameworks.

Investor implications

The divergence in ESG ratings is not a data error; it reflects fundamentally different methodologies, scopes, and weighting systems across providers. As a result, ESG scores should not be treated as objective or directly comparable indicators of sustainability performance.

For investors, this has three key implications:

  1. ESG ratings are directional, not absolute: A single ESG score does not fully capture a company’s sustainability profile. Differences between providers highlight that ratings depend heavily on what is measured—risk exposure, disclosure quality, or actual impact.
  2. Provider choice must align with investment strategy: Different rating agencies emphasize different dimensions. Risk-focused investors may rely more on Sustainalytics, while long-term sustainability positioning may align better with MSCI or CDP. Selecting a provider without understanding its methodology can lead to misaligned decisions.
  3. Multi-source analysis is essential for robust decision-making: Relying on a single ESG rating introduces blind spots. A combined view across multiple providers offers a more balanced understanding and reduces the risk of misinterpreting sustainability performance.

The practical lesson is simple: using only one provider can create blind spots, while triangulating across several providers produces a more reliable view.

Conclusion

ESG ratings should be used as analytical inputs,not decision outputs. Investors who understand the structure behind these scores are better positioned to identify real sustainability leaders versus reporting-driven performers

The most useful question is not which rating is correct, but what each rating is actually measuring.
